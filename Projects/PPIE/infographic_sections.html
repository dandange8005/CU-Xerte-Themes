Definitions of Involvement, Engagement and Participation in the health and social care sector

View text alternative

Involvement is about working with patients and the public. It's a collaborative effort where their perspective and experiences integrated into research processes or education design and delivery.

Engagement focuses more on raising awareness, sharing knowledge and findings. It is a two-way process, involving interaction and listening, with the goal of generating mutual benefit.

Participation is about recruiting people to take part in a study. Participants may be asked to complete questionnaires, partiipate in focus groups, or undergomedical procedures as part of research

Embedding PPIE in our research, education & training: why PPIE matters in academia

View text alternative

“PPIE helps us break down the often intimidating ‘ivory tower’ imagery that is sometimes associated with universities and helps us make a positive impact in the communities in which we work”

Regulators Requirements

Professional statutory regulatory bodies (PSRB) increasingly recommend PPIE in curriculum design and delivery and validation/revalidation of our education and training.

Achieving Impact

Embedding real-world experiences helps translate our findings into policy and practice, which achieves impact. It also helps to communicate our work outside the usual environments of academia, business and government.

Funder Requirements

Funders have prioritised PPIE activity and made it a key criterion for funding success. Writing high quality PPIE plans is integral to high quality grant submissions.

Meets Society's Needs

PPIE plays a vital role in strengthening the relevance and responsiveness of our research, education and training - it ensures we address society’s evolving needs and explore things that matter to them.

Prepared Students

Provides real experiences and context that complements theoretical and practical knowledge. It helps to prepare students for real world settings and introduces them to lived experiences.

Aligning to Policy

Legislation and accepted standards promote the value of PPIE e.g. Wellbeing of Future Generations (Wales) Act 2015 and the UK Standards for Public Involvement.

Our Civic Mission

We have a responsibility to connect and engage with wider society, to support openness and transparency, increase accountability and help build public trust in the work that we do.

Staff Development

PPIE supports beneficial personal growth and skills development in key areas such as communication skills. It can also be fun and rewarding.

Location: Module 1 - The importance of PPIE in academia

Our Four PPIE Principles

View text alternative

Our four PPIE principles are:

Meaningful

Activities should go beyond tokenism. They must be thoughtfully designed to ensure that public and patient contributors play a genuine role in shaping research and education.

Ethical

Activities must respect the rights, autonomy, and dignity of all participants, ensuring that involvement is conducted with transparency, honesty, and fairness.

Inclusive

Activities should aim to offer inclusive opportunities that support diverse representation of the voices you wish to engage and involve.

Impactful

Activities should generate a positive impact and result in tangible benefits for all including researchers or educators, and patients, the public or communities.

What does PPI impact look like across the research cycle?

View text alternative

Real examples taken from across CBLS Learning Case Studies to show PPI impact across the research cycle.

Research prioritisation

"When working with underserved communities to identify research priorities for the Wales COVID-19 Evidence Centre, these groups identified priorities that were not identified by the other stakeholders (e.g. policy makers, NHS leads). If we had not reached out to these groups, we would have missed key topics of our work programme - topics that mattered most to the public in the pandemic recovery" from Dr Natalie Joseph-Williams (DPM, MEDIC)

Planning the research

"When planning a study with people with learning disabilities, they pointed out that the primary outcome used in all trials to date in that area (looking at anger) were staff reported, and no one ever counted what they said about what they felt. This resulted in a key change to the trial, changing the outcome to self-reported." from Professor Kerry Hood (CBLS)

Applying for funding

"The feedback from the public involvement was acted upon to influence study aims, design, and language used in the application to make sure that the project was relevant and needed and had potential for societal impact for the underserved population that is the focus of the research." from Dr Katy Hamana (HCARE)

Conducting research

"Jan and Sue’s input around the cognitive interviews was particularly crucial. Jan kindly let me do a pilot cognitive interview with her, allowing me to better formulate and streamline the prompts and questioning. This was very useful for a very inexperienced interviewer like myself, and meant that the interviews all generally went smoothly." from Dr Tim Pickles (Centre for Trials Research)

Analysis and interpretation

"Patient & public partners helped challenge assumptions that may have otherwise gone unquestioned, such as the idea that digital interventions were universally accessible or that ‘participation’ looks the same for everyone. In turn, their contributions aided a more ethical, responsive, and grounded project that considered this during analysis." from Hannah Trotman (HCARE)

Sharing the findings

"Myself and one of the young committee members were invited to present at the Diabetes UK Annual Professional Conference in Glasgow about the committee and what we plan to do, as well as what the benefit has been to research. We showcased a video featuring our committee members about why they got involved, what they want to achieve from this experience and what advice they would give to researchers/clinicians." from Dr James Pearson (DII, MEDIC)

Making an impact

"Through our co-produced work and our lobbying, CACNA1C was added to the NHS PanelApp genomic screening, and global genomic testing and healthcare resources were updated for OMIM, Invitae, OpenHealth, and Unique." from Dr Jack Underwood (NMHII, MEDIC)

Evaluation and reflection

"We worked with our Public Partnership Group (PPG) at the Health and Care research Wales Evidence Centre to reflect on centre processes, including the ways in which we capture PPI impact. The PPG identified the challenges of using an existing impact tool in the rapid evidence environment, and helped us to adapt the tool so that it was fit for use in these settings." from Dr Denitza Williams, (DPM, MEDIC)

Planning Your Engagement - The 5Ws

View text alternative

When planning an engagement activity, it is helpful to respond to the 5Ws.

Who? Who do you want to engage?

Think about different demographics; the different hats people wear as well as different attitudes, perceptions, interests and experiences people have.

Why? What do you hope to achieve? (Raise awareness). What is the purpose of your engagement? (Inspire others to action).

When? When will the engagement take place? Think about the accessibility of your activity. What access requirements do you need to consider?

What? What activities will you use? Think about your specific audience’s needs. What ways are the most appropriate to provide a positive experience?

Which? Which methods will you use to evaluate the engagement? How am I going to prove my ‘why?

Engagement Do's and Don'ts: Your Guide to Quality Engagement

View text alternative

Some top tips compiled by experienced PPIE professionals, public contributors and researchers.

Do's

Know your audience - Tailor activities to different backgrounds, interests and experiences

Plan ahead - Structure sessions but leave room for questions, breaks and the unexpected

Make it interactive - Encourage dialogue through stories, visuals, demonstrations and hands-on activities

Show relevance - Connect discussions to everyday life and local issues, use relatable examples

Be open and transparent - Share your goals, expectations and limitations

Follow-up and reflect - assess outcomes, capture learnings and keep participants informed of next steps

Inspire others - Train colleagues, utilise your talents and interests, play to your strengths

Seek support - Ask for advice, undertake training, work in a team

Don'ts

Don't overload people - avoid jargon, stick to key messages, keep the scope and content realistic

Don't pretend - Try not to play a role, admit your own shortcomings

Don't be unethical - Respect privacy, safeguarding and sensitive topics

Don't dominate the conversation - Listen and give participants space to share

Don't dismiss criticism - Treat tough questions as constructive, ask for feedback

Don't take a one-size-fits all approach - Adapt to different groups and needs

Don't exclude anyone - Ensure accessibility across culture, disability and technology

Embrace the do's, steer clear of the don'ts, and embark on your own journey of successful engagement !

EDI in PPIE - Key legal duties & ethical frameworks

View text alternative

There are several legal duties underpinning EDI in PPIE

Equality Act 2010

Provides the legal foundation for EDI across public life. It protects individuals from discrimination, harassment, and victimisation on the basis of protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage & civil partnership, pregnancy & maternity, race, religion or belief, sex and sexual orientation.

Public Sector Equality Duty (PSED)

Requires public bodies (including Universities and research organisations) to eliminate discrimination, advance equality of opportunity, and foster good relations between different groups.

Human Rights Act 1998

Embeds European Convention on Human Rights into UK law, ensuring rights such as dignity, respect, and participation.

Health & Social Care Act 2012

Places duties on NHS organisations to involve and consult with patients and the public.

There are also several ethical frameworks that underpin EDI in PPIE

Research Ethics Principles (e.g. UKRI, NIHR, HRA)

Respect for persons: recognising dignity, autonomy, and lived experience. Justice: fair inclusion, avoiding exploitation or exclusion. Beneficence and non-maleficence: maximising benefits, minimising harms, especially for underserved groups.

UK Standards for Public Involvement

Inclusive opportunities, working together and support & learning are key ethical commitments of the standards.

Health and Care Research Wales - Equality Action Plan

Actions and activities that will embed inclusive practice across Health and Care Research Wales.

NIHR Race Equality Framework & Research Inclusion Strategy 2022-2027

The Race Equality Framework is a tool to help organisations deliver the change needed to address systemic inequities in health and care research. The Research Inclusion Strategy serves as a road map for implementing inclusive practice in research, culture and systems.

Framework to help deliver the change needed to address systemic inequities in health and care research.

Common barriers to EDI in PPIE

View text alternative

Structural and organisational barriers include :

Lack of diversity in recruitment – involvement opportunities often reach the “usual suspects” rather than underrepresented groups.

Inaccessible processes – systems not designed for people with disabilities, language barriers, or limited digital access.

Insufficient funding for inclusion – lack of budget for interpreters, accessible materials, carer costs, computers, or transport support.

Inflexible institutional policies – rigid times, venues, or formats that don’t accommodate diverse needs.

Limited organisational commitment – EDI is sometimes seen as an “add-on” rather than embedded in research culture and leadership priorities.

Practical and accessibility barriers include:

Timing and location – activities scheduled during working hours or in inaccessible places.

Digital exclusion – reliance on online engagement tools that not everyone can access or navigate.

Complex information – use of technical, academic, or medical language that excludes non-specialists.

Unclear roles or expectations – contributors may not understand their purpose or influence, leading to disengagement.

Insufficient feedback – contributors not told how their input has shaped outcomes, causing feelings of tokenism.

Cultural and social barriers include:

Lack of trust – historical or cultural mistrust of research institutions, particularly among marginalised communities.

Perceived power imbalances – contributors may feel their voices are undervalued compared to professionals.

Underrepresentation of lived experience – dominance of certain voices can unintentionally exclude others (e.g. by ethnicity, age, socioeconomic background, disability, gender identity).

Cultural insensitivity – poor awareness of cultural norms, communication preferences, or religious considerations.

Individual and psychological barriers

Low confidence or perceived lack of expertise – individuals may feel “not qualified” to contribute to research discussions.

Fear of stigma or judgement – especially for people with certain health conditions or personal experiences.

Language barriers – lack of translated materials or interpretation support.

Lower levels of literacy - lacking confidence to engage with materials

Previous negative experiences – poor treatment in earlier involvement projects discourages future participation.

Key steps that can help you to identify which groups are missing

View text alternative

Step 1 - Map the current participant landscape

Review your data: Look at who has been involved in your PPIE activities so far. Note demographics such as age, gender, ethnicity, socio-economic status, geographic location, disability, health condition, and language spoken.

Identify patterns: Are certain groups consistently absent or underrepresented?

Step 2 - Compare with the target population

Understand the population affected: Who is the research intended to benefit? Who’s voices do you need to hear from?

Compare representation: Identify gaps by comparing your PPIE participants with the actual diversity of the population. For example, are older adults represented if the research concerns age-related conditions? Or are minority ethnic groups included if they are disproportionately affected by the health condition that your engagement event is about?

Step 3 - Use data and evidence

Demographic and epidemiological data: National or regional statistics can help highlight groups at risk or historically underserved.

Health inequalities reports: Many public health agencies publish insights on groups who face barriers in accessing care or participating in research.

Step 4 - Engage key stakeholders

Community organisations: Local charities, advocacy groups, and social services often know which groups are underserved e.g. The Gateway Pavilion in Cardiff.

Existing patient advisory boards: They can provide insight into who is missing and why.

Community gatekeepers and leaders: are key to facilitating access and designing appropriate PPIE activities

Step 5 - Explore barriers and facilitators to involvement

Accessibility issues: Timing, location, transport, digital exclusion, or disability accommodations.

Cultural or linguistic barriers: Are materials available in different languages or culturally sensitive formats?

Trust and engagement issues: Historical marginalisation or negative experiences with healthcare/research may make some groups reluctant to participate.

Learning points to consider when supporting people from lower socioeconomic backgrounds in PPIE

View text alternative

1. Community outreach and education

Collaborating with local organisations to raise awareness about PPIE and its importance can help inform underrepresented communities

2. Building trust through relationships

Partnering with community leaders and organisation can help foster trust and facilitates more meaingful engagement

3. Inclusive recruitment processes

Using multiple channels, including community events and social media, tailored to each group's needs, ensures broad reach

4. Flexible involvement options

Offering various ways to engage, such as virtual meetings or small group discussions, can accomodate different schedules and preferences, while incentives can address finanical barriers

5. Feedback and adaptation

Gathering feedback from participants and adjusting approaches demonstrates a commitment to inclusivity.

By engaging individuals from low socioeconomic backgrounds, research becomes more reflective of real-world concerns, ensuring relevant outcomes and improving the impact of cancer research. These efforts can foster a research environment where inclusive, meaningful PPIE thrives.
